Treuhandstelle Interview Prep  ·  Topic 2 of 6

MII, MIRACUM & NUM

Germany's medical informatics infrastructure — the ecosystem your team operates inside.

Germany's university hospitals sit on vast stores of patient data — diagnoses, lab results, medication histories — but historically this data lived in siloed systems that could not talk to each other or be used for research at scale. The Medizininformatik-Initiative (MII) was launched in 2016 with federal funding from the BMBF to solve exactly this problem. Its goal: make health data from university hospitals interoperable, findable, and legally usable for research across institutions.

To achieve that, the MII organised Germany's 36 university hospitals into four regional consortia. Frankfurt's Universitätsmedizin belongs to MIRACUM (Medical Informatics in Research and Care in University Medicine), a consortium of 11 partner sites. At the national level, the Netzwerk Universitätsmedizin (NUM) — launched in 2020 initially to coordinate COVID-19 research — has become the permanent infrastructure layer for cross-site clinical research networks. All three share the same philosophy: standardise data (using HL7 FHIR and MII Core Data Set profiles), build shared Datenintegrationszentren (DIZ), and make that data available for approved research through trusted, governed access — which is precisely where the Treuhandstelle comes in.

The Treuhandstelle at Universitätsmedizin Frankfurt is the identity and consent management node inside a larger ecosystem: the DIZ handles data integration, MIRACUM sets consortium-wide data governance rules, and MII defines the overarching framework (including what consent forms must contain and how pseudonyms are structured). As Leitung, you will need to:

  • Align the THS's consent and pseudonymisation workflows to the MII Core Data Set and MIRACUM specifications.
  • Represent Frankfurt's Treuhandstelle in national MII and NUM governance bodies.
  • Coordinate with the DIZ on data release requests — deciding what can be shared, with whom, and under what conditions.
  • Adapt local processes when MII/MIRACUM update their standards (which they do regularly).

The interviewer will bring this up because it reveals whether you understand the structural context of the job, not just the technical tools.

Level 1 Basic awareness — can define it
  • MII = Medizininformatik-Initiative; BMBF-funded since 2016, connecting 36 German university hospitals across four consortia.
  • The four consortia are MIRACUM, HiGHmed, SMITH, and DIFUTURE.
  • Frankfurt's hospital belongs to MIRACUM (11 partner sites).
  • NUM (Netzwerk Universitätsmedizin) is a parallel national network for clinical research coordination, originally stood up for COVID-19.
  • The shared technical standard is HL7 FHIR with MII Core Data Set profiles.
Level 2 Working knowledge — can describe how it works
  • Each MII hospital runs a DIZ (Datenintegrationszentrum) that extracts, harmonises, and stores data in FHIR format according to MII modules (Person, Diagnose, Prozedur, Medikation, Laborbefund, etc.).
  • The MII Core Data Set (Kerndatensatz) defines the minimum structured data every DIZ must provide — the common denominator for cross-site queries.
  • Patient consent is managed by the Treuhandstelle using the MII-standard broad consent form (Modellvorhaben Einwilligung); gICS implements the logic.
  • Pseudonymisation is handled by gPAS; the pseudonym links the DIZ record to the consented patient without exposing identity to researchers.
  • Data use requests go through a Data Access Committee process; the THS validates consent and provides pseudonymised linkage keys.
  • MIRACUM runs a federated query infrastructure (FDPG — Forschungsdatenportal für Gesundheit) where researchers can query data across all consortium sites simultaneously.
Level 3 Applied understanding — connects to job tasks
  • When MII updates its broad consent model (e.g. adding new use-case policies), the THS must update gICS policy trees and re-audit impacted consent records — this is your team's operational responsibility.
  • MIRACUM consortium governance meetings are where cross-site interoperability decisions are made; you would represent Frankfurt's THS, reporting conflicts or gaps to the consortium.
  • NUM projects (e.g. NAPKON, CODEX) required rapid consent and data release at scale in 2020–2021 — this experience shaped current German THS best practices; knowing this history matters for credibility.
  • The FDPG portal relies on the THS having correctly structured pseudonymisation so that cross-site record linkage is possible — a configuration error at THS level breaks the entire distributed query.
  • Budget justification for THS headcount and infrastructure is easier when framed against MIRACUM deliverables — know how to use consortium obligations as internal leverage.
Level 4 Interview-ready — can handle tough questions
  • Can articulate the tension between federated and centralised data models, and explain why MII chose federated — political trust, legal jurisdiction, GDPR data minimisation.
  • Can explain what happens when a patient withdraws consent mid-project: which systems are involved, in what order, and what the THS obligation is under MIRACUM rules.
  • Can name at least two ongoing MIRACUM research projects and explain what data they use and what THS involvement looks like.
  • Can describe the distinction between the DIZ role and the THS role — who controls what, why separation of functions matters for data protection, and how they collaborate without merging.
  • Can critique current MII/MIRACUM pain points (e.g. heterogeneous consent implementations across sites) and propose what Frankfurt's THS should do about them.

Answer strategy
Emphasise the separation of functions: DIZ handles data (extraction, harmonisation, storage); THS handles identity (consent, pseudonyms, linkage keys). This separation is not just organisational convenience — it is a data protection requirement, since combining both functions in one unit would undermine the independence of identity management. Describe the workflow interface: THS validates consent, provides pseudonym keys; DIZ releases data.
Answer strategy
This is a process question — they want to see systems thinking. Cover: (1) THS receives withdrawal, updates gICS — consent record flagged; (2) THS notifies DIZ to restrict pseudonymised data; (3) Downstream projects with active data use are notified; (4) Already published/anonymised data is generally not affected — explain why (legal basis); (5) The audit trail in gICS is the legal proof the withdrawal was processed. Answer strategy
Three reasons to hit: (1) Legal — each hospital is the data controller under GDPR; sending data to a central pool would require additional legal basis; (2) Political trust — hospitals and state governments were unwilling to cede control of patient data to a central authority; (3) Data minimisation (GDPR principle) — federated queries return only aggregate or de-identified outputs, reducing exposure. Note the trade-off: federated models are technically harder and slower for some use cases.

Die deutschen Universitätskliniken verfügen über riesige Mengen an Patientendaten — Diagnosen, Laborbefunde, Medikationsverläufe. Diese Daten lagen jedoch jahrzehntelang in voneinander isolierten Systemen, die weder miteinander kommunizieren noch für die Forschung im großen Maßstab genutzt werden konnten. Die Medizininformatik-Initiative (MII) wurde 2016 mit Förderung des BMBF ins Leben gerufen, um genau dieses Problem zu lösen. Ihr Ziel: Gesundheitsdaten aus Universitätskliniken interoperabel, auffindbar und rechtssicher für die institutionsübergreifende Forschung zu machen.

Dazu wurden Deutschlands 36 Universitätskliniken in vier regionale Konsortien organisiert. Das Universitätsklinikum Frankfurt gehört zu MIRACUM (Medical Informatics in Research and Care in University Medicine), einem Konsortium von elf Partnerstandorten. Auf nationaler Ebene ergänzt das Netzwerk Universitätsmedizin (NUM) — 2020 ursprünglich zur Koordination der COVID-19-Forschung gegründet — die MII als permanente Infrastrukturschicht für standortübergreifende klinische Forschungsnetze. Alle drei — MII, MIRACUM, NUM — folgen derselben Grundidee: Daten standardisieren (per HL7 FHIR und MII-Kerndatensatz-Profilen), Datenintegrationszentren (DIZ) aufbauen und diese Daten über geregelte Zugangswege für die Forschung nutzbar machen — genau hier setzt die Treuhandstelle an.

Die Treuhandstelle am Universitätsklinikum Frankfurt ist der Identitäts- und Einwilligungsknoten innerhalb eines größeren Ökosystems: Das DIZ übernimmt die Datenintegration, MIRACUM gibt die konsortiumsweiten Datenschutzregeln vor, und die MII definiert den übergeordneten Rahmen — einschließlich der Anforderungen an Einwilligungsformulare und die Struktur von Pseudonymen. Als Leitung werden Sie folgende Aufgaben wahrnehmen müssen:

  • Einwilligungs- und Pseudonymisierungsworkflows an den MII-Kerndatensatz und die MIRACUM-Spezifikationen anpassen.
  • Die Frankfurter Treuhandstelle in nationalen MII- und NUM-Gremien vertreten.
  • Datenfreigabeanfragen gemeinsam mit dem DIZ koordinieren — entscheiden, was unter welchen Bedingungen an wen weitergegeben werden darf.
  • Lokale Prozesse anpassen, wenn MII oder MIRACUM ihre Standards weiterentwickeln (was regelmäßig geschieht).

In MIRACUM-Gremien, wo die meisten Teilnehmenden entweder Kliniker oder IT-Ingenieure sind, bringt eine Apothekerin mit Informatikkompetenz eine seltene Hybridperspektive auf Medikationsdatenqualität ein — ein Modul des MII-Kerndatensatzes.
